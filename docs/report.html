Introduction:

Many consider the 21st century as the best time to be human as we are afforded luxuries and lifestyles that have never before been so accessible. But is this world safer? As technology and society develop, income inequality has skyrocketed (The Hunger Project). The media and news cycle show their viewers a world that is increasingly more dangerous, less safe, and rife with criminal activity. Our research seeks to answer the following research questions: 

Research Question(s):

Has the world become more violent in the past 15 years, and if so, how does this breakdown across region? Given this, does GDP correlate with these observations?

Our hypothesis is that contrary to portrayals in media, much of the world is actually becoming less violent. We also hypothesize that volatile and unstable regions of the world, whether that be from civil conflict, interstate conflict, eroding rule of law, or political strife, are responsible for a large share of the violent crimes and homicides that fold into the worldwide statistics. This research will also allow us to empirically evaluate a 1976 Department of Justice claim regarding the often-cited observation that GDP and violent crime are correlated, if only at the macro level. Given this claim, we believe that as a region’s GDP increases, their rates of violent crimes decrease.  

The homicide and crime data were originally curated by the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (ODC) Homicide Statistics. The ODC compiles national and international data on homicide from a variety of sources (including criminal justice and public health systems from the countries included and the World Health Organization) which include data that conform to the ODC’s definition of crime, that being ’’ the punishable contravention or violation of the limits on human behavior as imposed by national criminal legislation.” Homicides are defined as the intentional killing of one person by another by the UN. The data is recorded in 5-year intervals with the first publication being in 2005 and the most recent being 2020. There are ethical concerns resulting from the fact that every one of those millions that make up the statistics of deaths and crime are still personal tragedies.

The GDP data shows the GDP of each country through the years 1995-2020 ( in approx. 5 year intervals), demonstrating how many countries have managed to increase their GDP substantially. Additionally, these data sets are merely factual representations of the economic growth of a given nation and as such did not pose any discernible threat to the individuals in these countries. Since no civilian was impacted by the methods used to obtain the data, there are no ethical concerns.

Methodology:

For the data analysis, we intend to utilize visualizations of the regional and global homicide statistics to derive our findings as related to our first question. For the latter half of our question, we will be utilizing a series of regression models to determine the level of correlation between homicides and GDP via their R^2 values.

# | label: total-gdp-homicides

gdp_un_total <- gdp_un |>
  filter(Region.Country.Area.GDP == "1") |>
  filter(SeriesGDP == "GDP in current prices (millions of US dollars)")
gdp_un_total$ValueGDP <- as.numeric(as.character(gdp_un_total$ValueGDP))

#Crime
homicides_and_crime_un_total <- homicides_and_crime_un |>
  filter(Region.Country.Area == "1") |>
  filter(Series == "Intentional homicide rates per 100,000") 

#GDP
gdp_current_and_crime_total <- homicides_and_crime_un_total |> 
  full_join(gdp_un_total, by = "Year") |> 
  filter(!is.na(Region.Country.Area)) 

modeltotal <- linear_reg() |>
  set_engine("lm") |>
  fit(ValueGDP ~ Value, data = gdp_current_and_crime_total)

Results:

Overall, our findings were the following:

RQ1 - Global and Regional Statistics

Based on our initial findings, our hypothesis is supported by the UN data. The global homicide rates have been consistently declining on 5-year intervals. Breaking the statistics down regionally, we find that Asia, Europe and Africa are likely responsible for this observation on account of their massive share of the global population. Surprisingly, North America saw a large spike in homicides since 2010, possible on account of the 2008 financial crisis and its impact on the middle class in the US.

RQ2 - Global - By Sex

Broken down by sex, it seems that men are much more likely to be victims of homicide than women. This is unsurprising, not only due to the known likelihood of men being involved in violent/aggressive activity but especially considering that the global population breakdown is 101.68 men per 100 women as of 2020.

RQ3 - Regressions - Regional and Global

Overall, based on our initial findings, there is mixed correlation between GDP and homicides. The global homicide and GDP statistics had a very high R^2 value (0.93). Breaking the statistics down regionally, we find that Asian and European statistics have high R^2 values, African and Latin American statistics have middling R^2 values, and Oceanian and North American statistics have very low R^2 values. These results call into question the application of the Justice Department’s claim that GDP and violent crime are correlated, but we do lack the full scope of the data needed to fully evaluate the claim.
